## Understanding the Quality of Life Scale

When a beloved dog faces a serious illness or the natural decline of aging, pet parents often struggle with one of the most difficult questions: **"How do I know when it's time?"** The Quality of Life (QoL) scale provides a compassionate, structured framework to help you assess your dog's daily experience and make informed decisions with your veterinary team.

## The HHHHHMM Scale Explained

Developed by veterinary oncologist **Dr. Alice Villalobos**, the HHHHHMM scale evaluates seven essential dimensions of your dog's well-being. Each factor is scored on a scale of 0 to 10, where 10 represents the best possible condition.

### Hurt (Pain Management)

Is your dog's pain being adequately managed? Consider whether your dog can rest comfortably, whether they flinch or cry when touched, and whether pain medications are providing relief. Adequate pain management is the foundation of quality of life.

### Hunger (Appetite)

Is your dog eating enough to maintain their body condition? A declining appetite can signal pain, nausea, or progression of illness. Hand-feeding, appetite stimulants, or dietary modifications may help temporarily.

### Hydration

Is your dog drinking enough water? Dehydration can cause significant discomfort and accelerate decline. Subcutaneous fluids administered at home can help maintain hydration for dogs who aren't drinking adequately.

### Hygiene

Can your dog be kept clean and comfortable? Incontinence, non-healing wounds, or the inability to groom can significantly impact a dog's dignity and comfort. Regular bathing, padded bedding, and diaper options can help.

### Happiness

Does your dog still show interest in life? Look for tail wags, engagement with family members, enjoyment of favorite activities (even modified ones), and responsiveness to affection. Mental engagement is as important as physical comfort.

### Mobility

Can your dog get up and move around? Consider whether they can reach their food, water, and bathroom area. Mobility aids like harnesses, ramps, and orthopedic beds can extend comfort for dogs with limited movement.

### More Good Days Than Bad

When bad days consistently outnumber good days, quality of life has likely declined below an acceptable level. Keeping a daily journal helps you track this ratio objectively rather than relying on memory alone.

## How to Score Your Dog

Rate each of the seven categories on a scale of **0 to 10**:
- **0** = Very poor, maximum suffering
- **5** = Acceptable, manageable
- **10** = Excellent, normal function

**Total the scores.** A combined score above **35 out of 70** generally suggests acceptable quality of life. Scores consistently below 35 may indicate that your dog's suffering outweighs their comfort.

> **Important:** The scale is a guide, not a verdict. No single number can capture the full picture of your dog's experience. Use it as one tool among many in your conversations with your veterinarian.

## When to Seek Professional Guidance

If your assessments show a declining trend, or if you're unsure how to interpret the results, reach out to your veterinarian or a [pet hospice](/pet-hospice/) specialist. Many veterinarians offer quality-of-life consultations specifically to help families navigate this process.

If you're considering [at-home pet euthanasia](/at-home-pet-euthanasia/), a quality of life assessment can help you feel confident that you're making a compassionate, well-informed decision for your dog.
